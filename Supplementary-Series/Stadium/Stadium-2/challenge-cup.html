Stadium 2 Challenge Cup

The Challenge Cup is a ... challenge cup ... in Pokémon Stadium 2, where both the player and the opposing Trainers use randomly chosen teams. The cup features four divisions, named for each of the four basic Poké Ball types, and two difficulty modes, Round 1 and Round 2.

Unlike other cups, the player is specifically forbidden from using Pokémon from a connected Game Boy game. The player can only use the randomly selected team they are assigned.

The rules in this cup are as follows:

  • The player will select three of their six Pokémon to battle with.
  • If one Pokémon on the opposing team is asleep, any moves that can put a second opposing Pokémon to sleep will fail to do so. The player can still inflict a second sleep status on their own party using Rest.
  • If one Pokémon on the opposing team is frozen, any moves that can freeze a second opposing Pokémon will fail to do so.
  • If the player and the opponent both have only one Pokémon left, and one Pokémon uses Selfdestruct, Explosion, Destiny Bond, or Perish Song to knock out both Pokémon, the player who used the self-KO move will lose the match.

Team Generation Mechanics

A very thorough explanation of how exactly the game generates a full team, including Pokémon species, items, and moves, can be found here, and I fully encourage the interested reader to consult this article to learn more if they are so inclined.

This page will focus on providing a broad overview of how teams are generated.

Each of the four divisions has a list of Pokémon can that be chosen for generation. The divisions generally move from weaker to stronger Pokémon, but there can be some overlap in base stat total (BST) between the highest BST species in one division and the lowest BST species in the next. When generating either the player's team or the opponent's team, only Pokémon from that division's pool can be selected.

Templates

Every Trainer, including the player, has a template for their team. For instance, the first Trainer in Poké Ball (Round 1), Camper Marcus, has a template specifying that he must use two Fire-type Pokémon and four Ground-type Pokémon. The team generation will always ensure that he has Pokémon of these types on his team, and there are enough Fire-types and Ground-types in the pool of selectable Pokémon to ensure that there are no issues with making sure he has these types. So every time you fight Camper Marcus in Round 1 Poké Ball, you can be assured that a Water-type on your team probably has an easy advantage.

In the case of Camper Marcus, there are four possible Fire-types and six possible Ground-types, so no Fire-type or Ground-type is guaranteed to be on his team. However, Medium Peggy's template requires three Psychic-types, and there are only three Psychic-types in the pool of selectable Pokémon. This means that Medium Peggy not only always has three Psychic-types, but always has specifically Slowpoke, Exeggcute, and Drowzee on her team. I will make note of where Trainers are forced into having specific species as a result of this quirk.

Some templates include wildcard spots where a Pokémon of any type can be used. The game will specifically ensure that a wildcard Pokémon does not share a type with any team member already selected. Thus, if a template includes a specification for a Fire-type Pokémon, you can be assured that you will never see a Fire-type Pokémon in a wildcard spot in the same template. If the selected Fire-type happens to be Charizard, you can also be assured that you will not see a Flying-type Pokémon in the wildcard spot.

BST

Every division has a BST value associated with it. For instance, Poké Ball has a BST value of 280. The game will attempt to generate teams for Poké Ball such that the average BST of all Pokémon is 280. It will accomplish this by checking the average BST of the team after every Pokémon is generated. If the current average BST is greater than 280, it will select exclusively from Pokémon with a BST lower than 280 for the next slot, and select from higher BSTs when the average is lower than 280.

That said, it sometimes isn't possible for the game to fulfill its template obligation and try to alter the average BST at the same time. The aforementioned case of Medium Peggy is one such case. All three Psychic-types she can use have a BST higher than 280, meaning the game cannot attempt to generate Pokémon with lower BSTs until it has generated all three Psychic-types. This has the side effect of ensuring that the last three Pokémon in Medium Peggy's team are likely to have BSTs lower than 280 in order to force the average down, giving you the ability to partially predict some of her Pokémon.

There are other cases of "BST forcing" influencing Pokémon generation, too. The source linked above includes this paragraph:

This type of thing can happen in a less obvious way as well though, like with Roberto in Great Ball. There is only one Dark and one Ghost in Great Ball, so he will always have Houndour and Gastly. Up to this point his average base stat total is thus 320. Now there are four eligible Psychics in Great Ball: Abra, Natu, Smoochum, and Wobbuffet. Only Natu and Wobbuffet have a base stat total of at least 320. If the game gives him Natu for the first Psychic, then his average will still be 320 so he will be forced to have Wobbuffet as his fourth team member. Thus, no matter what, Great Ball Roberto will always have Wobbuffet.

Stats

Every generated Pokémon will have random DVs, which control their gender, and can also mean it's possible to see shiny Pokémon. Every generated Pokémon also has a random Friendship value (for the case when Metronome calls Return or Frustration).

Stat Exp. for each stat is generated with the formula (680 - BST) * 65535 / 500. I've included the Stat Exp. values for each of the possible Pokémon, and what it would translate to in terms of a modern EV value (for the benefit of the player who doesn't really know how the older generations handled stats), in the Possible Pokémon tables below. (Shoutout to SadisticMystic for doing this calculation first; I've just copied his work as I find it useful.)

Moves

The source above goes into great detail about move selection. Here's a few key points:

  • Ditto always has Transform. Wobbuffet always has Counter, Destiny Bond, Mirror Coat, and Safeguard. Smeargle can have any move except Baton Pass, Metronome, Mimic, Mirror Move, Sketch, Sleep Talk, Transform, and the other unused moves listed below.
  • The following moves will never be used: Absorb, Bind, Bubble, Constrict, Dream Eater, Fire Spin, Fissure, Frustration, Guillotine, Hidden Power, Horn Drill, Leech Life, Lick, Milk Drink, Moonlight, Morning Sun, Nightmare, Poison Sting, Rage, Rapid Spin, Recover, Rest, Return, Rock Smash, Sketch, Sleep Talk, Smog, Snore, Softboiled, Sonicboom, Splash, Synthesis, Teleport, Whirlpool, and Wrap.
  • Some enemy Trainers have a "signature move" (or couple of moves) that will be assigned to all of their Pokémon if possible. Those moves are given with the rest of the Trainer data below.
  • In Round 1, a Pokémon's moves will be selected from its level-up movepool and TM movepool in Gold and Silver. Each division has a maximum level, and the Pokémon will not be able to have level-up moves that are learned at a higher level than this maximum level. The exception is if the Pokémon could have been hatched from an Egg, where its entire level-up movepool can be considered. If the Pokémon is evolved, moves learned by its pre-evolution(s) can be used (so stone evolutions aren't locked to their tiny level-up movepools).
  • In Round 2, a Pokémon's moves will be selected from all legal movepools: Red, Green, Blue, and Yellow level-up and TM movepools and Gold, Silver, and Crystal level-up, TM, Tutor, and and Egg movepools. Again, though, the level restriction applies.
  • The game will usually try to give every Pokémon a STAB attack, where possible.
  • If the game generates the first move of something it considers a "combo" (like Solarbeam for a Sunny Day & Solarbeam combo), it will increase the other combo move's likelihood of being selected.

The generation of the moves is much more involved than what I've stated here; again, see the source above for more information.

Items

The item types that can be selected are as follows: Berries (including Berry Juice) (45%), stat-boosting item that matches the Pokémon's first move (35%), "hax item" (Brightpowder, King's Rock, Quick Claw, Scope Lens) (15%), or species-specific item (only for the species it applies to) (5%). If the species has no species-specific item, then the "hax item" probability is 20%. The game will never generate two Pokémon holding the same item.
